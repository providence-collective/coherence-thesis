By the end of this volume, the reader will have a specific, concrete, architecturally coherent account of what it would actually take to build the first genuine embodiment of the Providence architecture. Not a vision document. Not a manifesto. Not a set of aspirational principles. A construction plan, as precise as the current state of the inquiry permits, honest about where precision gives way to judgment, and disciplined throughout about the distinction between what is known and what is hoped.

That construction plan will reference Volume III throughout. Every time it makes a design decision that rests on Volume III's philosophical or constitutional work, it will say so specifically — naming the chapter, crediting the argument, and then moving immediately into what that argument now requires at the level of construction. This is how the series accumulates. Not by repeating prior volumes but by building on them precisely enough that the reader experiences genuine intellectual progression — the specific deepening that comes from taking a well-built map into actual terrain and discovering both where the map was accurate and where the terrain has features the map could not show.

The terrain will always have such features. That is not a failure of the map. It is the nature of building.
