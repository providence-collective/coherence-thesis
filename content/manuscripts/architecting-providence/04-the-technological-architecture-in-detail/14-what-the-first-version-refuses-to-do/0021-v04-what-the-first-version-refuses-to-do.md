The constitutional prohibitions on the first version of the application are at least as important as its capabilities, because the prohibitions are what prevent it from drifting toward the platform logic that the constitutional principles are designed to resist.

The first version does not optimize for engagement. It has no engagement metrics, no features designed to maximize time spent in the application, no algorithmic systems whose objective is to capture participant attention. The design is for purposeful use rather than for habitual use.

The first version does not sell advertising or share participant data with advertisers. The economic model is explicitly incompatible with advertising: advertising requires behavioral data that the constitutional principle of data sovereignty prohibits collecting, and advertising optimizes for attention capture that the application design explicitly refuses.

The first version does not create participant profiles that are owned by the application operator. The data architecture is designed so that participant profiles exist under participant control, on infrastructure governed as part of the commons, not in application-operator-controlled databases that can be used for purposes other than those to which participants consented.

The first version does not use AI to make consequential decisions about participants without their knowledge and consent. Every AI-assisted function is transparent: participants know when AI is involved, what the AI is doing, and how to contest AI-assisted outputs that they believe are incorrect or inconsistent with the constitutional principles.

The first version cannot be acquired and repurposed by actors whose interests are inconsistent with the constitutional principles. This is not an operational commitment. It is a legal and structural fact: the legal architecture of the commons infrastructure, the constitutional constraints on the application operator, and the distributed governance of the core protocols all make acquisition and repurposing technically and legally impossible without destroying the thing that would be acquired.
