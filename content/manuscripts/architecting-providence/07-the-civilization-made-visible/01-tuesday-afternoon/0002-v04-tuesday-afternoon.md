*An architecture is tested not by the situations it was designed for, but by the ordinary afternoons it never anticipated.*

Begin with the unremarkable. Most of what happens inside any living institution is not a crisis. It is the steady, textured, often tedious work of people coordinating their lives — and the quality of an institution is determined more by how it handles the ordinary than by how it performs in the dramatic moments that get remembered. This chapter describes an ordinary afternoon in a founding community, not because the ordinary is where the architecture's claims are most ambitious, but because it is where they are most easily falsified.

The community in this chapter is a composite, drawn from the design requirements of Chapter Two and given enough specificity to be imaginable. It does not exist. It is a thought experiment in the strict sense — an attempt to reason carefully about what the architecture would actually produce by imagining it in operation. Where the architecture handles the situation well, the chapter says so. Where it struggles, the chapter says that too, because the struggles are more instructive than the successes.
