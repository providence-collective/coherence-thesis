The deepest open question of Chapter Twenty-One — and of this volume — is whether the people who will build Providence can sustain the intellectual and psychological honesty that the enterprise requires over the full duration of the building. The analysis of Volume III and IV has demanded a form of honest reckoning with difficulty and uncertainty that is genuinely demanding to maintain under the pressures of the actual building process. The founding culture must establish and protect this honesty as a constitutional requirement, because the alternative — the drift toward the comfortable optimism that makes difficult work feel more certain than it is — is one of the most reliable paths to the partial and catastrophic failures this chapter has named.
