Partial failure is the most likely failure mode. It is the failure in which Providence achieves some of its aims while being captured or distorted in others — in which the constitutional architecture holds in some domains and is violated in others, in which some communities genuinely embody the design principles while others drift toward something the design was intended to prevent.

Partial failure is not catastrophic in the way that complete failure is. Some good is done. Some communities live better because of the architecture. Some coordination is achieved that would not have been achieved without it. But partial failure is also not success. And partial failure is particularly difficult because it provides the cover of genuine achievement that can obscure the failure that is occurring in other domains.

The design against partial failure is the constitutional audit culture: the regular, honest assessment of whether the architecture is functioning as designed across all of its dimensions, not only the dimensions where it is most visibly succeeding. The audit culture is hard to maintain because the pressures — organizational, financial, cultural — consistently favor attention to success over attention to failure. The founding culture's establishment of honest self-assessment as a core practice is the primary protection against partial failure becoming invisible.
