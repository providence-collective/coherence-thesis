We have argued that the body cannot easily fake presence — that the autonomic signatures of genuine encounter are difficult to counterfeit by will. This is true at the level of a single moment. But over time, with incentives in place, a subtler failure becomes possible: not the crude faking of signal, but the genuine reshaping of the self into a performer of coherence. People are extraordinarily adaptive to what is rewarded. A civilization that rewards presence might produce, not more genuinely present people, but more people who have trained themselves into the physiological appearance of presence — who have learned to produce the regulated voice, the coherent heart rhythm, the calm and open affect, as a kind of deep performance that is no longer quite a lie and no longer quite the truth.

This is the failure we have called the Heisenberg problem in its most developed form: the measurement does not merely disturb the measured, it slowly remakes it. The result would be a kind of coherence theater performed at the level of the nervous system itself — a civilization of people so practiced at being present that presence has become their most sophisticated mask, the genuine encounter it was meant to protect hollowed out from inside by the very attention meant to honor it.

So here is what we are already doing about it. We are building the system to work primarily after encounters rather than during them, so that no one sits performing for a visible meter while trying to be present; we are building in deliberate measurement-free periods that preserve a protected refuge of unmeasured experience; and we are pointing the reflective questions inward, toward the participant's own truth, rather than outward toward any score to be optimized. But the deepest thing we are working on is harder than architecture, and we are working on it deliberately: the cultivation of a culture, held within the network itself, that prizes genuine encounter over its appearance so fiercely that the performance of presence comes to feel like the betrayal it is. We do not yet know whether such a culture can hold against the gravity of incentives — but we are treating its cultivation as core engineering, not as an afterthought, because we understand that the subtlest failure of all is the one no sensor can catch, and that the only instrument capable of detecting performed presence is a community that genuinely loves the real thing more. Building that community is among the first things we are setting out to do.
