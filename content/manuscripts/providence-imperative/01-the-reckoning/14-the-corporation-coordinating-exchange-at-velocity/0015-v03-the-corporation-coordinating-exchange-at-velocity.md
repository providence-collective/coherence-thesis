The modern corporation is perhaps the most efficient coordination mechanism ever created for a specific purpose: the aggregation and deployment of capital toward the production and distribution of goods and services at scale and speed. It built the railroads, electrified the cities, produced the vaccines, and created the supply chains that feed and clothe the world's eight billion people.

What it broke is now visible in the planetary systems that its products have stressed beyond their limits. The corporation's fundamental logic — aggregate capital, deploy toward highest return, externalize costs that cannot be captured — is structurally incapable of accounting for the value of things that cannot be priced: a functioning atmosphere, a living river, a community's coherence, a future generation's inheritance. The deeper problem is that the corporation has colonized not just the economy but the imagination — so that we now reflexively reach for corporate structures when we want to build anything at scale, even when what we are trying to build is precisely the thing that corporate logic destroys.

What must be preserved: the capacity for decisive action, for the aggregation of diverse capabilities toward a common productive end, for the creation of durable value at scale.
