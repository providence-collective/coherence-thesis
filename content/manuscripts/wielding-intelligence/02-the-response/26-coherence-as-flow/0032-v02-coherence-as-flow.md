This reframes what coherence might mean in an economic context. An earlier framing in this book described coherence as something like capital. That framing has problems. Capital is an instrument of accumulation. Coherence does not accumulate well; it degrades when stockpiled, the way trust does, the way relational depth does, the way attention does. A more accurate framing is that coherence is a property of circulation. It exists in the patterns by which value moves through a system, not in the units the system counts.

A healthy economy is one in which value circulates among the conditions that sustain it. A regenerative agricultural economy circulates value back into the soil that produces the food, the laborers who tend the soil, the communities whose health depends on the food, and the ecological systems whose regulation depends on the agriculture. An extractive agricultural economy moves value outward—away from the soil, away from the laborers, away from the local community, toward distant shareholders and financial intermediaries. The total wealth generated may be similar in the short term. The pattern of flow is entirely different, and over time the patterns produce entirely different outcomes.

This is what coherence as flow means structurally. It is not a score attached to an individual. It is a property visible in the architecture of how value moves through a system over time. Whether a community is regenerating or depleting itself is observable through the circulation patterns of its currencies—financial, social, ecological, relational. The patterns are visible without requiring intimate surveillance of any individual participant.

This matters because it changes what kinds of instruments are needed. The question is no longer “how do we measure individual coherence and reward it?” The question is “what coordination infrastructures support circulation patterns that regenerate the conditions communities depend on?” These are different design problems, and the second one is much more tractable than the first.
