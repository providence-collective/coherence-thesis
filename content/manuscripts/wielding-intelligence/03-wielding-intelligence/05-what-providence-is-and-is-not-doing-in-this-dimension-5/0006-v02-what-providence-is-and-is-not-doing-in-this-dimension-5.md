The same discipline that has governed the previous chapters applies here. Providence is not a builder collective. It does not represent or speak for the people working in any of the worlds named above. It is not claiming alignment with projects it has not been in direct relationship with. The projects named in this chapter—OpenCivics, the Capital Institute, ReFi DAO, the Inner Development Goals, the Mind & Life Institute, the contemplative-sustainability researchers, the AI alignment community, the Indigenous-led conservation initiatives—are doing their own work on their own terms, and the book’s mention of them is description rather than affiliation.

What Providence is being designed to do, at its current early stage, is operate at the layer Chapter Five described: helping aligned actors find one another in the matching architecture, supporting them in meeting one another in small groups, and developing over time the trust infrastructure and coordination substrate that more ambitious coordination would require. The bet is that an enormous amount of latent coordination capacity already exists in the people working in these adjacent worlds, and that simply helping them find one another at higher density than current architectures permit would produce value that the current institutional landscape cannot generate.

This is the modest claim. Providence is not the field. It is one attempt to build coordination infrastructure adequate to the field. The field exists with or without it, and the field will continue to develop whether or not this particular project succeeds. What Providence is trying to contribute is the kind of relational infrastructure through which the field can begin to find itself.
